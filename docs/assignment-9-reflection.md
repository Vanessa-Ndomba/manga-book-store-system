# Assignment 9 Reflection

Designing the domain model and class diagram for the MangaBookStore system was one of the most important steps in translating my earlier analysis artifacts (requirements, use cases, Agile planning, and behavioral models) into a coherent object-oriented structure. Unlike earlier assignments where the focus was on describing what the system should do, this task required me to decide how the system should be represented internally through classes, attributes, methods, and relationships. The biggest value of the exercise was that it forced me to think carefully about boundaries, responsibilities, and the real-world business rules that drive the platform.

One of the main challenges I faced was deciding the correct level of abstraction for the domain model. An online manga store includes many possible entities: users, sessions, catalog items, inventory records, orders, payments, shipments, reviews, discounts, wishlists, and many more. However, the assignment required only 5–7 entities. That meant I had to prioritize the concepts that are both core to the business and strongly supported by the earlier assignments. I used the functional requirements and the main user journeys (browse/search, add to cart, checkout, payment, and order tracking) as the guiding criteria. This is why I focused on entities such as UserAccount, MangaListing, ShoppingCart, CartItem, Order, and PaymentTransaction. I also included UserSession because session timeout and secure authentication are explicitly part of the requirements, and session behavior was already modeled in Assignment 8.

Another challenge was defining relationships correctly (association vs. aggregation vs. composition) and ensuring the multiplicities made sense. For example, I needed to decide whether a ShoppingCart “owns” CartItem objects strongly enough to be composition. In practice, cart items should not exist without the cart; they are created when a user adds an item and removed when the cart is cleared or converted to an order. This makes composition an appropriate choice because the lifecycle of CartItem is fully dependent on ShoppingCart. In contrast, MangaListing objects exist independently of carts and orders, so the relationship from CartItem to MangaListing is better represented as a reference/association.

I also struggled with method definitions at first. It was easy to list attributes (title, price, quantity, status), but harder to decide what operations truly belong inside each class, especially when thinking in terms of real implementation. To solve this, I revisited the use cases and extracted the most important actions. For example, “Add Book to Cart” and “Cart modifications” map naturally to ShoppingCart methods like addItem(), removeItem(), updateQty(), and calculateTotal(). “Checkout” maps to converting a cart into an Order and initiating a PaymentTransaction. “User Login” and “Logout” map to UserAccount and UserSession responsibilities such as authenticate(), start(), refresh(), and end(). This approach ensured the class diagram was not arbitrary: it was derived from system behaviors already documented.

Alignment with previous assignments was an important part of my design decisions. The requirements document lists concrete business constraints (session timeout, cart persistence, stock validation, and specific order statuses). Assignment 8 already modeled object states for the shopping cart, order, and payment transaction, so the class diagram needed to include those classes and support those transitions through methods. For example, the Order class has a status attribute (Processing/Shipped/Delivered/Cancelled), which directly supports the state transitions and the order management requirements. The PaymentTransaction class includes a status (Pending/Authorized/Failed/Refunded) which reflects the payment transaction state model and allows alternative flows such as payment failure and retry.

A significant trade-off was simplifying the inventory and fulfillment area. The system requirements include admin inventory management and the behavioral modeling includes inventory and shipment state diagrams. In a full-scale domain model, I would likely introduce InventoryItem and Shipment as first-class entities and potentially separate catalog representation (MangaListing) from inventory representation (InventoryItem with stockQuantity and restock rules). However, given the entity limit and the focus on the customer purchase workflow, I prioritized the purchase and payment path. This trade-off helped keep the model clean and focused but also highlighted how real systems often require more classes than an academic deliverable allows.

The biggest lesson I learned from this assignment is that object-oriented design is about organizing responsibility, not just drawing boxes. A good class diagram should communicate how the system will behave, what rules it enforces, and how data flows through different lifecycles. Multiplicity and relationship type matter because they express business constraints: one user may place many orders, a cart contains multiple cart items, and an order has a payment transaction. By building the domain model first and then turning it into a Mermaid class diagram, I gained a deeper understanding of how requirements become structure, and how structure supports maintainability and clarity. Overall, this assignment strengthened my ability to model real-world systems in a way that is consistent, traceable, and aligned with earlier analysis work.
